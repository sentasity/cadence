Left to its own devices, Claude jumps straight to code. It skips the design, leaves plans half-done, and forgets to validate. The traditional answer is to catch the gaps yourself by reading every diff. But when AI writes most of the code, line-by-line review stops being a realistic place to put your attention.

Cadence is built for that new reality.

## The bet: spend more time on design, less on implementation

Cadence is an **AI-first workflow**. The bet it makes is that with a capable model writing the code, the scarce resource is no longer typing: it's *direction*. So Cadence flips where your time goes: you spend significantly **more** time on the front half (brainstorm → design → plan) and much **less** worrying about how any of it is implemented.

When implementation is fast and cheap, brainstorming and intent refinement become the bottleneck, and a workflow that treats them as afterthoughts squanders everything the model is good at. You still work by intent ("I want a credit ledger that shows AWS credits per account"), but instead of letting that intent slide straight into an unreviewable pile of code, Cadence routes it through stages, each with a written artifact and an explicit gate where *you* decide whether to continue.

You direct judgment at the gates. The machine carries the verification load.

<Aside type="note" title="Who this is for">
Anyone building software with AI. Cadence isn't about your skillset: even developers who *can* read every diff shouldn't have to spend their attention there. The age of reviewing every single line is behind us; the future is intent-driven. Cadence lets AI own the low-level specifics while you stay focused on business case, UI/UX, and the other high-level calls that actually need you.
</Aside>

## Why gates and readable artifacts are the whole point

Once you stop reviewing line by line, "the tests pass" or "here's the diff" isn't trust: it's a leap of faith. Cadence replaces the leap with two things you *can* evaluate at the level of intent:

- **Readable artifacts at every stage.** Brainstorm produces a plain-English stub. Design produces a narrative you can actually follow. The plan is exact, but the design it came from speaks your language. You're never asked to approve something you can't understand.
- **Explicit gates between stages.** Nothing advances on its own. Brainstorm stops and tells you to run design. Design stops and waits for you to say "approved." Execute stops at the audit. Each pause is a place where your judgment (the thing the machine still needs from you) is exactly what's called for.

The mental model is simple: **you supply intent and judgment at the gates; the artifacts let you exercise that judgment without reading a single line of code.**

## How the trust gets built without you reading diffs

Two stages exist specifically so you don't have to inspect the implementation:

- **Audit** checks the work *against its own plan*: every checkbox backed by real code, no half-finished steps, no quietly-dropped scope, no deferred `TODO`s smuggled in. It's an automated reviewer that answers "did it actually do what the plan said?"
- **Validate** walks the work *as a user would* after deploy: the manual and automated checks that confirm the thing behaves the way you wanted.

Together they carry the verification load. You judge intent at the design gate; audit and validate confirm the result matches that intent. That's the chain that lets you trust what shipped without ever reading a diff.

<Aside type="tip" title="The pause is the feature">
Every "now run the next command yourself" is deliberate. The friction *is* the safety. A workflow that chained all six stages automatically would be faster, and would put the result back out of your reach.
</Aside>
